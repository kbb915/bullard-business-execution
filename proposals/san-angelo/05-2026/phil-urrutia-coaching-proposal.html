The Problem

The first six months have four traps to avoid.

The coaching challenge is pacing the real tensions of the transition: "outsider," "fixer mentality," "listener," "purposeful actual change," "prioritization," and "unification."

Trap 1Outsider Speed

Moving too quickly can make the right changes feel imposed.

"I'm gonna be coming in as an outsider and that's always a tough situation especially in a smaller town."

The coaching work is to notice when "fixer mentality" is helping and when it needs to be turned off long enough to listen, learn the departments, and understand the local context.

Trap 2Passive Listening

Listening alone will not satisfy the work council expects early.

"There may be some things that the council wants to maybe get their hands dirty on right off the bat."

The recommendation is not passive listening. It is a weekly structure for holding both sides of the work: "be a listener" and bring purposeful actual change.

Trap 3Unprioritized Change

The organization may not be able to absorb every improvement Phil can see.

"There's gotta be a little bit more prioritization on what the organization can handle."

The engagement turns that into a practical filter for early decisions: what gets moved now, what waits, and what needs more trust before the organization can absorb it.

Trap 4Tenacity

Phil's Working Genius of Tenacity is a gift, but it has to be aimed carefully.

Pat Lencioni's Working Genius language gives us a useful coaching lens: Tenacity helps Phil finish, follow through, and keep pressure on what matters. In a new city manager role, that same gift can become costly if it locks onto the wrong issue too early, or if the right issue is pursued before allies and stakeholders are ready to move with him.

About The Work

This is executive coaching for transition judgment, not generic leadership encouragement.

The engagement is designed around the decisions Phil will need to make in real time: when to listen, when to act, how to work through the "50-50 mix" he described, how to build a true city management team, how to handle retirements, and how to aim Tenacity at the right thing at the right time. The coaching will also keep returning to commitments: what he is committed to, what story he is living from, and whether his action is aligned with the trust he is trying to build.

The PhilosophyTrust and traction are both first-six-month outcomes.

The work is to reduce guessing.

The transition includes "a 50-50 mix," council items that need attention "pretty quick off the bat," and the need for "prioritization on what the organization can handle." Coaching gives those questions a weekly place to become decisions.

The RoleA thinking partner for the new seat.

I will help Phil turn observations into sequence.

The value is not a script. The value is disciplined reflection tied to action: what Phil is hearing, what needs patience, what needs movement, and what must be sequenced so purposeful actual change does not outrun "what the organization can handle."

The WatchpointTenacity needs timing and target discipline.

The coaching will monitor where Phil's drive is pointed.

Tenacity can help Phil bring purposeful actual change. Early in the role, it needs a checkpoint: is this the right issue, the right moment, and the right pace for "what the organization can handle"?

The Commitment LensWhat Phil is choosing to lead from.

Phil thinks in commitments, so the coaching should meet him there.

The Plan

Twelve months of coaching: weekly through the transition, then bi-weekly as the work settles in.

The first six months follow the real rhythm of entry: each month has a leadership question, a practical focus, and a decision discipline so the transition does not become either overcorrection or drift. The second six months continue bi-weekly so the habits, team cadence, and planning-readiness work hold after the initial intensity passes.

What's At Stake

What happens if the first six months are left to instinct alone.

The risks are practical: "outsider" entry, "fixer mentality," "council wants fixed," "what the organization can handle," and the need for "unification."

Risk 1

The fixer label forms before trust does.

If early change is not sequenced well, "fixer mentality" can show up before enough listening has happened. The coaching question is when to turn it on and when to turn it off.

Risk 2

Council's early priorities wait too long.

Listening is essential, but Phil also said "council wants fixed" and wants some attention "pretty quick off the bat." The first six months need a narrow lane for action that council can recognize.

Risk 3

The staff split hardens.

There is "a 50-50 mix": "don't bother me" on one side, and "more involvement" and "more dialogue" on the other. Both signals need to be managed intentionally.

Risk 4

Change fatigue arrives before alignment.

The standard here is "what the organization can handle." Prioritization protects the change, not just the people experiencing it. Trust brings alignment, so the first work is not only deciding what to change; it is building enough trust that people can align around the change.

Risk 5

Tenacity gets aimed at the wrong thing too soon.

If Phil's natural drive locks onto an issue before the relational and political context is ready, he may win motion but lose trust. The coaching relationship keeps asking: is this the right thing, the right time, and the right way to bring people with him?

Risk 6

A limiting story becomes the operating story.

If Phil starts living into a limiting story such as "I am the outsider" or "I have to prove value quickly," that story can quietly shape pace, tone, and priority. The coaching work is to keep the story visible and keep the commitment stronger than the pressure.

The Outcome

Six months from now, the transition should feel steadier; twelve months from now, it should feel embedded.

The goal is not to solve every San Angelo issue in the first six months. The goal is to stay disciplined around the transition priorities: "be a listener," purposeful actual change, "prioritization," and "unification." The second six months protect that work as Phil and the council move from negotiation and entry into sustained leadership.
